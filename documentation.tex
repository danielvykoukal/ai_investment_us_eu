\begin{abstract}
\noindent
This document explains, step by step, the empirical project behind a single
claim: \emph{Europe researches and uses artificial intelligence, but does not
build, patent, or fund it.} The evidence is a set of directly observable
quantities. On the \emph{production} side, the United States and China account
for virtually all frontier AI---models, leading developers, patents, compute,
and data centres---while Europe is near-absent. On the \emph{research} side,
Europe is genuinely strong: it is second in the world in AI research citations.
On the \emph{use} side, European firms and citizens adopt AI heavily and fast.
The result is a paradox: Europe does the science and the consuming, and even
leads on trust and regulation, while the capital and the companies that own AI
sit elsewhere. The document covers (i) the reproducible two-stage pipeline,
(ii) every data series with its source, units, and treatment, and (iii) the
eleven descriptive charts that carry the argument. The project is
\emph{descriptive by design}: these are measured magnitudes, not a latent causal
channel, so it runs no econometrics. The adoption series is pulled live from
Eurostat; the production, investment, infrastructure, public-money, and talent
figures are read from the Stanford AI Index 2026 and should be refreshed each
report cycle.
\end{abstract}

\section{Overview and research question}\label{sec:overview}
% ============================================================================

\paragraph{Thesis.} A common framing of the EU's position in AI fixates on the
venture-capital gap with the United States. This project widens and sharpens the
point into a structural contrast across the whole AI value chain. The claim
breaks into five legs, each mapped to one or more charts:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\Alph*)]
  \item \textbf{Funding.} Private capital for AI is overwhelmingly American, and
        the lead is \emph{accelerating}, not closing.
  \item \textbf{Production.} The frontier models, the leading developers, the
        patents, the data centres, and the chip supply chain are American or
        Chinese; no European company is among the top model builders.
  \item \textbf{Research.} Yet Europe is strong \emph{upstream}: it is second in
        the world in AI research citations and a major publisher. The science is
        European; the products are not---the project's central paradox.
  \item \textbf{Use.} European firms and citizens \emph{adopt} AI heavily and
        fast. Europe is a large, growing customer for AI it did not build.
  \item \textbf{Within-Europe divides \& talent.} Adoption is uneven (north-west
        vs south-east; large firms vs SMEs), and Europe's big economies are weak
        magnets for AI talent even though several hold talent well.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Why descriptive, not econometric.} The sibling precautionary-saving
project devotes most of its length to isolating a \emph{latent} causal channel,
because there co-movement is not causation. Here the situation is different:
every leg above is a \emph{directly measured} quantity---a count of models, a
flow of dollars, a share of firms, a count of data centres. The argument is an
accounting comparison across the value chain, not an identification problem.
There is no hidden variable to recover and nothing to instrument. The honest
altitude is therefore a set of clean, well-sourced charts; what the project owes
the reader is rigour about \emph{data provenance and comparability}, which
Section~\ref{sec:data} supplies.

\paragraph{What the documentation covers.} Section~\ref{sec:pipeline} describes
the pipeline. Section~\ref{sec:data} justifies every data choice and states the
comparability caveats that matter. Section~\ref{sec:descriptive} walks through
the eleven charts in narrative order. Sections~\ref{sec:interpretation}--%
\ref{sec:limits} synthesise and qualify the findings. The appendix lists the file
tree, commands, and a data dictionary.

\section{The reproducible pipeline}\label{sec:pipeline}
% ============================================================================

The project is split into two scripts by \emph{function}, so each stage can be
run, audited, and re-run independently. Data collection (network-bound,
non-deterministic across vintages) is separated from figure rendering (fast,
deterministic, offline). There is deliberately no third stage: per
Section~\ref{sec:overview} the analysis is descriptive.

\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.25}
\begin{tabular}{@{}p{4.4cm} p{4.4cm} p{5.8cm}@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Stage / script} & \textbf{Reads} & \textbf{Writes} \\
\midrule
\code{01\_collect\_data.py} & Eurostat (live API) + AI Index 2026 figures &
  \code{data/*.csv} (15 tidy series) \\
\code{02\_make\_figures.py} & \code{data/*.csv} &
  \code{figures/*.png} (11 charts, A--G) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\paragraph{One-command reproduction.} The wrapper \code{run\_all.sh} installs the
pinned dependencies and runs the two stages in order:
\begin{lstlisting}
bash run_all.sh        # pip install, then run 01 -> 02 (classic Python)
\end{lstlisting}

\paragraph{Classic Python, no virtual environment.} The project runs directly on
a standard system Python ($\ge 3.11$). \code{run\_all.sh} installs the
requirements into the chosen interpreter (\code{python3} by default; override
with \code{PYTHON}) and then runs the two stages. Dependencies are pinned in
\code{requirements.txt}, so the charts are reproducible across machines.

\paragraph{Network robustness.} The adoption data is pulled through the keyless
\code{eurostat} package. Each pull is wrapped in \code{try/except}: a failed
source prints a clear message and leaves any CSV from a previous run untouched.
The latest survey year is detected automatically from the returned table, so a
new Eurostat release flows through to the country and firm-size charts with no
code change.

\section{Data}\label{sec:data}
% ============================================================================

For each series we state the \emph{source} and dataset code, the \emph{units},
the \emph{coverage}, and any \emph{transformation} and \emph{caveat}. The
adoption series is free and keyless; the AI Index figures are public but, as the
report's underlying data is proprietary (Quid, Epoch AI, etc.), are read from the
report PDFs and entered as documented constants with their figure numbers---the
project's one set of ``verify before publishing'' numbers.

\subsection{Use: AI adoption by EU enterprises}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{description}[leftmargin=1.4em,style=nextline]
\item[Source / code] Eurostat \dataset{isoc\_eb\_ai}, indicator \code{E\_AI\_TANY}
  (enterprises using at least one AI technology), unit \code{PC\_ENT}, size class
  \code{GE10} (10+ persons employed). Pulled live.
\item[Coverage / units] Annual; available years 2021, 2023, 2024, 2025 (no 2022).
  Percentage of enterprises using $\ge 1$ AI technology---an \emph{extensive}
  measure (any use), not intensity or spend.
\item[Caveat: series break] Eurostat broadened the surveyed AI-technology list
  between the 2023 and 2024 waves; the EU-27 rate jumps $8.1\%\to 13.5\%$, partly
  a definitional effect. The $2024\to 2025$ step ($13.5\%\to 19.9\%$) is on the
  consistent definition. The trend chart shades the break.
\item[Firm size] The same series for \code{EU27\_2020}, split by size classes
  \code{10-49}, \code{50-249}, \code{GE250}.
\end{description}

% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Two comparability caveats that matter}\label{sec:caveats}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item \textbf{Incommensurable units, kept separate.} Adoption (a share of
  firms), investment (a flow of dollars), models (a count), and citations (a
  share) are never combined into one index; they are parallel lenses on the same
  gap. In particular, Eurostat firm adoption ($\approx 20\%$) and the McKinsey
  ``organisational adoption'' figure ($88\%$) measure different things and must
  be cited separately.
  \item \textbf{``Europe'' is defined differently across sources.} In investment
  it includes the UK; in models/research it is a regional aggregate; in data
  centres and talent it appears as individual countries. Each chart's caption
  states which.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Descriptive evidence}\label{sec:descriptive}

The eleven charts are intentionally simple; in narrative order---fund, build,
research, house, publicly fund, use, staff---they are the argument.

\paragraph{The funding gap.}
\projfig{0.82}{A_investment_by_geo}{\textbf{Leg A.} Private AI investment by
geography, 2025. The US bar runs off the field on purpose: at US\$285.9\,bn it is
$\approx 23\times$ China and $48\times$ the UK. Europe's leaders are slivers.}

\projfig{0.72}{A2_investment_gap}{\textbf{Leg A.} US vs Europe vs China,
2023--25. The US curve goes vertical (+$\approx 160\%$ since 2024) while Europe
(+$\approx 7\%$) and China stay near the floor. ``Europe'' includes the UK;
pre-2024 is an earlier report vintage.}

\paragraph{The production gap.}
\projfig{0.80}{C_models_by_country}{\textbf{Leg B.} Notable AI models by country,
2025 (Epoch AI). The US released 59 and China 35; \emph{all of Europe} released
2. Put beside the funding bar: US \$286\,bn buys 59 models; Europe's
$\approx$\$21\,bn, two.}

\projfig{0.82}{C2_developers_by_origin}{\textbf{Leg B.} The organisations
building frontier models, 2025, coloured by origin. Every one of the top twenty
is American, Chinese, or (for LG) South Korean. \emph{No European company
appears.}}

\paragraph{The paradox: strong in science, absent in products.}
\projfig{0.80}{D_europe_paradox}{\textbf{Leg C.} Europe's share of the global AI
pipeline. Upstream (research) it is large---$19.5\%$ of citations, $11.1\%$ of
publications; downstream (production) it collapses---$3\%$ of patents,
$\approx 2\%$ of notable models. Europe does the science, not the product.}

\paragraph{The physical backbone and the public purse.}
\projfig{0.80}{E_datacenters_by_country}{\textbf{Leg B.} Data centres by country,
2025 (Cloudscene). The US hosts 5{,}427, more than $10\times$ any other country;
ownership of the chip stack (Nvidia/TSMC/SK Hynix) is likewise non-European.
Counts ignore facility size.}

\projfig{0.66}{F_public_investment}{\textbf{Leg A.} Cumulative government AI
spending, 2013--24. Even public money is lopsided: US $\approx$\$20.5\,bn vs
European contracts $\approx$\$3.7\,bn. The two are measured differently (caption);
like-for-like contracts are US $\approx$\$4.6\,bn vs Europe $\approx$\$3.7\,bn.}

\paragraph{The consumer side: Europe uses AI heavily.}
\projfig{0.66}{B_adoption_by_country}{\textbf{Leg D/E.} AI adoption by EU country,
2025 (Eurostat). A north-west/south-east split: Denmark $42\%$ leads, Romania
$5\%$ trails---an eight-fold range around the $19.9\%$ EU-27 average.}

\projfig{0.74}{B2_adoption_trend}{\textbf{Leg D.} AI adoption over time. The EU-27
rate roughly doubled in two years. The $2023\to 2024$ segment is shaded because
the survey definition was broadened; $2024\to 2025$ is clean.}

\projfig{0.58}{B3_adoption_by_firmsize}{\textbf{Leg E.} EU-27 AI adoption by firm
size, 2025. Large firms ($55\%$) adopt at roughly three times the small-firm rate
($17\%$): the SME gap within Europe.}

\paragraph{Talent.}
\projfig{0.74}{G_talent_migration}{\textbf{Leg E.} Net AI talent migration, 2025
(LinkedIn). Small hubs (Luxembourg) attract strongly, but Europe's big economies
(Germany $+0.17$, Spain $+0.23$) barely do. The US still imports talent ($+1.2$)
though its inflow fell $89\%$ since 2017.}

\section{Interpretation}\label{sec:interpretation}
% ============================================================================

Putting the legs together:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Funding (A).} Unambiguous and widening. US private AI investment
  more than doubled in 2025 to US\$285.9\,bn; cumulatively since 2013 the US has
  attracted $\approx$US\$757\,bn against Europe's low tens of billions, and even
  public AI money is lopsided.
  \item \textbf{Production (B).} Starker still. Europe built two notable models in
  2025, holds $3\%$ of AI patents, and places no company in the top twenty
  developers; the data centres and the chip supply chain are American, Chinese,
  Taiwanese, and Korean.
  \item \textbf{Research (C).} The twist: Europe is genuinely strong upstream
  ($19.5\%$ of AI citations, second only to China), and its papers come
  disproportionately from academia. The science is European; the
  commercialisation is not.
  \item \textbf{Use (D) and divides (E).} Europe adopts AI heavily and fast---the
  EU-27 firm rate doubled to $\approx 20\%$ in two years, and population-level use
  is high---but unevenly (Denmark $42\%$ vs Romania $5\%$; large firms $\approx 3
  \times$ SMEs), and its big economies attract little AI talent.
\end{itemize}
The synthesis is the headline: \emph{Europe is integrating AI into its economy,
and doing much of the underlying science, while the capital, the companies, and
the infrastructure that \emph{own} AI accrue almost entirely elsewhere.} Europe's
distinctive strengths---research and, separately, trust and regulation---sit
upstream and sideways of the value chain, not at its commercial core. The
adoption gap is closing; the production and funding gaps are not.

\section{Limitations and caveats}\label{sec:limits}
% ============================================================================
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Adoption series break.} The $2023\to 2024$ jump partly reflects a
  broadened survey definition; lead with $2024\to 2025$ or annotate the break.
  \item \textbf{``Europe'' is not one thing.} It includes the UK in investment,
  is a regional aggregate in models/research, and appears as individual countries
  in data centres and talent (Sec.~\ref{sec:caveats}).
  \item \textbf{``Notable models'' is a curation.} Epoch AI's set is manual and
  author-affiliation-based (double-counting possible); read it as a pattern.
  \item \textbf{Data-centre counts $\ne$ capacity.} Cloudscene counts facilities,
  not size, power, or utilisation.
  \item \textbf{Public-spend methodologies differ.} US (FPDS obligations) and
  Europe (TED contract ceilings) are not directly comparable beyond contracts.
  \item \textbf{Talent nuance.} The 2025 data show Europe's big economies as weak
  \emph{magnets}, not a dramatic outflow; the sharp move is the $89\%$ fall in US
  inflow. Do not overclaim a European ``brain drain.''
  \item \textbf{Two adoption scales.} Eurostat firm adoption ($\approx 20\%$) and
  McKinsey organisational adoption ($88\%$) measure different things.
  \item \textbf{Vintages.} The adoption series is pulled live and revised by
  Eurostat; the AI Index figures are hand-entered from a periodically updated
  report. Regenerate and re-verify before citing any number.
\end{itemize}
